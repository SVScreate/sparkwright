### My Assessment: The Name Is Yours to Register

The legal standard for trademark conflicts is **likelihood of confusion** — would a reasonable consumer be confused about whether the two products come from the same source? The answer here is clearly **no**, and here's why:

| Factor | sparkwright.org (you) | sparkwright.ai (them) |
|---|---|---|
| Industry | Educational software/tools | B2B AI phone services for trades |
| Customer | Homeschool parents, tutors, ed therapists | HVAC contractors, plumbers |
| Channel | Consumer web, educational market | Tradespeople, service businesses |
| Purchase context | Parent/teacher buying learning tools | Business owner buying answering service |
| Price point | Low-cost digital education tools | Business software subscription |

There is no consumer overlap. An HVAC contractor shopping for a phone answering service will not confuse it with a math practice game. A homeschool parent will not confuse a math game with a business phone tool. These marks can coexist legally.

**The .ai vs. .org distinction is a secondary point** — what matters is the goods and services, not the domain. But the completely different industries are the core argument.

### One Thing to Know

Coexistence now doesn't mean it's locked forever. If sparkwright.ai ever expands into education — or you ever expand into B2B services — the analysis could change. That's unlikely, but it's worth noting. The time to register is now, before either party grows significantly.

## Trademark Registration — Plain-English Walkthrough

The USPTO process has 6 steps. Here's what they actually mean for you.

### Step 1: Is trademark right for you?

**Yes.** You have a distinctive coined word ("Sparkwright" doesn't describe educational tools — it's invented), you're using it commercially (or about to), and you want exclusive rights to it in your industry. That's exactly what trademark is for.

**"Sparkwright" is a strong mark.** Invented/coined words are the strongest category of trademark — stronger than descriptive words like "Quick Math" or "EduGames." That's good news for registration and for enforceability later.

### Step 2: Get ready to apply

You need to decide three things:

**A. Trademark format — what are you registering?**
- **Standard character mark** — just the word "Sparkwright," in any font, style, or size. Protects the word itself, not the logo. This is the most powerful protection.
- **Stylized/design mark** — your specific logo design. Protects that exact look.
- **Recommendation: File a standard character mark first.** It protects the word broadly. You can file a design mark later once Pip's logo is finalized. Two separate applications, two separate registrations — but word mark protection is more valuable and you can file it now.

**B. Goods and services — what classes?**
Trademark is organized into 45 International Classes. You need to pay per class. Two are relevant:

- **Class 41 — Educational services:** Online educational games; educational software; tutoring; educational testing services. This is your primary class — the games, the platform, the instructional content.
- **Class 9 — Computer software:** Downloadable educational computer software. This covers the software itself if you ever sell it as a download or app.

**Filing both is ~$500–700 in government fees** (TEAS Plus = $250/class, TEAS Standard = $350/class). Filing only Class 41 is ~$250–350 and covers the most important ground. See fee section below.

**C. Filing basis — use vs. intent to use**
- **"Use in commerce" (1a):** You've already made a commercial sale. You have a specimen (screenshot of the product being sold with the name visible).
- **"Intent to use" (1b):** You haven't made a first commercial sale yet, but you genuinely intend to. No specimen required at filing — you provide it after your first sale.

**Since you're pre-launch: file on Intent to Use (1b).** This is exactly the right path. It lets you lock in your priority date now, before launch. After your first sale, you file a Statement of Use (SOU) with a specimen. You have 6 months from the Notice of Allowance to file the SOU, with up to 5 extensions (for a total of 3 years) — plenty of runway.

**Priority date matters:** Whoever files first gets the earlier priority date. Filing before launch is smart even though you can't claim use yet.

### Step 3: Prepare and submit your application

Filed through **USPTO Trademark Center** at USPTO.gov. You need a USPTO.gov account with two-factor authentication. The application itself is a form — you fill in:
- The mark (type "Sparkwright" for a standard character mark)
- The classes and description of goods/services
- Filing basis (1b — intent to use)
- Your name and contact info (this becomes public record)
- The filing fee (paid by credit card)

**TEAS Plus vs. TEAS Standard:**
- TEAS Plus ($250/class): Requires you to pick your description of goods/services from the USPTO's pre-approved ID Manual. More restrictive but cheaper.
- TEAS Standard ($350/class): Lets you write a custom description. More flexible, costs more.

For "educational games" and "educational software," there are good pre-approved descriptions in the ID Manual, so TEAS Plus is likely available and saves money.

**You're responsible for monitoring your application status** throughout the process. Check every 3–4 months in the Trademark Center "Drafts and docket" section. Missing a deadline = application abandoned. This is a recurring calendar task.

### Step 4: Work with the examining attorney

After you file, a USPTO examining attorney reviews the application. This takes several months. They'll check:
- Does it meet minimum requirements?
- Are there any conflicting marks in the database?
- Is the description of goods/services acceptable?

If there's an issue, they send an **office action** — a letter explaining the problem. You must respond within **3 months** (extendable for a fee to 6 months). If you don't respond, the application is abandoned.

Common office actions for clean applications like this one: description of goods/services needs clarification. Usually fixable.

If the examining attorney finds a conflicting mark (none currently exists for "Sparkwright"), they'd issue a likelihood-of-confusion refusal. You'd then argue coexistence — that's where a lawyer becomes much more valuable.

### Step 5: Approval and publication

If approved, the mark is published in the **Trademark Official Gazette** for 30 days. Anyone who believes they'd be harmed by your registration can file an opposition. sparkwright.ai *could* file an opposition here — but they'd have to argue likelihood of confusion, which, as analyzed above, is a weak argument given the completely different industries.

If no opposition (or if opposition fails): you receive a **Notice of Allowance** (since you're filing 1b). Then you have 6 months to file your Statement of Use (once you make your first sale).

### Step 6: Maintain your registration

This is the part people forget. Registration doesn't last forever automatically — you have to file maintenance documents:

| When | What | Consequence of missing |
|---|---|---|
| Between years 5–6 | Section 8 Declaration (still in use) + optional Section 15 (incontestability) | Registration cancelled |
| Between years 9–10 | Renewal (Section 9) | Registration expires |
| Every 10 years after | Renewal | Registration expires |

**Put calendar reminders for year 5 and year 9 immediately after you get your registration certificate.** This is the most common way small business trademarks die — the owner forgets the maintenance window.

You're responsible for enforcing your own trademark. USPTO won't police it for you — they just give you the legal rights. If someone infringes, you have to act.

## On the Attorney Question

The USPTO explicitly says US-based filers are **not required** to have an attorney. They "strongly encourage" it — but that's their standard language, and it was written before AI tools changed the research and drafting landscape significantly.

Here's my honest take:

**Where you don't need an attorney (for this filing):**
- Your mark is clean — coined word, distinctive, no conflicts in TESS
- The coexistence situation with sparkwright.ai is straightforward — different industries, no confusion argument
- The filing itself (TEAS Plus, standard character mark, Class 41) is a fairly standard form
- With AI assistance, you can get the goods/services description right, which is the most common source of DIY errors

**Where you might want one:**
- If you get an office action that's more than a simple description clarification (e.g., a likelihood-of-confusion refusal)
- If sparkwright.ai files an opposition after publication
- Once you're generating real revenue and the trademark has business value worth protecting

**My recommendation:** File it yourself using TEAS Plus. The risk is low, the mark is clean, and the cost savings are real. What you need from me before you file is:
1. The exact goods/services description from the USPTO ID Manual
2. A review of your completed draft application before you submit
3. A plain-English answer to any question that comes up during the form

Save the attorney budget for the moment you need to respond to something adversarial (office action, opposition). That's when the expertise pays for itself.
