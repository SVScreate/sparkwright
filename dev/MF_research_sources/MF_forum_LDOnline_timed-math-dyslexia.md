## Summary

A parent of a second-grade dyslexic child seeks advice before an IEP meeting. The child has an IEP with extended time accommodations, but the teacher is still administering standard timed math tests and believes they are necessary for memory development. The thread surfaces a clear tension: timed exercises as a *practice tool for building automaticity* vs. timed tests as *graded competitive assessments*. One commenter articulates this distinction precisely — and it maps directly onto Math Flash's design philosophy.

## Posts

**Original Post — Anonymous (undated, thread opens April 4, 2003)**
Parent of a 2nd-grade dyslexic child. The child has made progress in reading and math skills but struggles with timed math assessments. The IEP includes extended time accommodations, but the teacher believes timed tests are necessary for memory development. The parent — also dyslexic — recalls similar frustration from their own second grade experience. The child's self-esteem is declining; math homework that used to take 30 minutes now takes 2 hours. Parent seeks guidance on advocating at an upcoming IEP meeting.

**Anonymous — Wed, 02/12/2003**
Recommends written communication before meetings: *"a letter is never ignored"* and creates documentation valuable if legal action becomes necessary. Notes the school is violating the IEP if it specifies extended time accommodations.

**Anonymous — Wed, 02/12/2003** *(original poster responding)*
Will prepare a written outline of concerns, observations, and accommodation recommendations for the Friday meeting. Notes the teacher failed to implement promised resources during the school year.

**Anonymous — Wed, 02/12/2003**
Advises focusing the meeting discussion narrowly on the IEP violation rather than philosophies about timed tests generally. Suggests requesting 50% extended time as the *"standard accommodation."* Notes that dyslexic students read test content more slowly, affecting performance on timed assessments.

**Anonymous — Fri, 02/14/2003**
Describes implementing reduced problem quantities with accuracy-based grading (*"19/20 completed = 90%"*). Notes the child's production increased over time while maintaining focus on accuracy rather than speed.

**Anonymous — Mon, 02/24/2003**
Uses the term *"noncompliance"* regarding IEP violations. Recommends documenting all conversations in writing. Notes that processing delays warrant different performance expectations than peers without such delays.

**Anonymous — Tue, 02/25/2003**
Reports a child achieving 100 math problems for the first time. The child recognized personal improvement despite cognitive processing differences in different operations.

**Ken — Tue, 02/25/2003**
Defends timed exercises as valid measurement tools for building automaticity in math facts. Argues the problem lies in *implementation* rather than the strategy itself: *"competition should be based upon previous individual performance."*

**Anonymous — Wed, 02/26/2003**
> **Key quote:** *Distinguishes between timed exercises for building automaticity versus graded timed tests. Notes that graded timed tests administered competitively "totally undermines the value of math timings" and is unfair to students with processing differences.*

**Beth — Fri, 04/04/2003**
Describes intensive home intervention using *"skip counting to a metronome"* over six months to build multiplication fact automaticity, achieving significant improvement in both calculation skills and self-esteem preservation.

## Key Signals for Math Flash

- **The distinction that matters:** Multiple commenters understand intuitively that *building automaticity through timed practice* is different from *graded competitive timed testing*. Math Flash lives entirely in the first category — this thread shows that educators and parents who understand this distinction are the right audience.
- **Processing speed ≠ not knowing:** The parent's core insight — her daughter knows the facts but can't output them under time pressure — is exactly the problem Math Flash's accommodation threshold (item 130) is designed to address.
- **Self-esteem is the collateral damage:** "Math homework now takes 2 hours instead of 30 minutes" and declining self-esteem are downstream effects of misapplied timed pressure. Math Flash's "no score pressure during Practice Quest" design directly responds to this.
- **IEP context:** Families in this space are often navigating formal accommodations. A tool that documents the threshold used in its print report (e.g., "Fluency assessed at 5s threshold — accommodation for processing speed") aligns with IEP documentation practice and could be genuinely useful to these families.
- **"Competition should be based upon previous individual performance"** — Ken's quote from 2003 is a nearly word-for-word description of what Math Flash does. Students are measured against their own history, not against a class average or fixed standard.
- **50% extended time (1.5×) cited as "standard accommodation"** — consistent with the 1.5× convention documented in the Session N accommodations research. A 5-second threshold (≈1.67× of 3s) sits in this range.
