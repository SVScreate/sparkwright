## Notes on Review Pool

- The overwhelmingly negative skew is consistent with review site dynamics — satisfied users rarely seek out review pages. These are frustration voices, not a representative sample.
- The two ADHD-identified students (Joey T. and Penny M.) provide the clearest window into what the accommodation-aware audience experiences. Both describe anxiety/stress specifically in the context of the timer.
- Several reviewers are clearly very young (2nd/3rd graders writing in their own voice). This is the student, not the parent, speaking — unusual and notable.
- The complaints about the character ("Mr. C," "egg man," "bowling ball") are worth noting: the character creates anxiety in some students and is a source of ridicule. Math Flash has no punishing character. This is a quiet differentiator.

## Key Signals for Math Flash

- **ADHD students are hit hardest by the timer pressure** — two students identify themselves and both describe stress/panic responses. This directly validates the accommodation threshold feature (item 130).
- **"I spam random numbers"** (Joey T., ADHD) — this is the worst-case outcome of timed pressure: the student stops trying to retrieve and just fires random answers. The learning has completely stopped. Practice Quest is the opposite of this response.
- **"You only get 3 SECONDS TO ANSWER! Then its 2.6 then its 1.5!"** (Penny M., ADHD, 5th grade) — she's describing XtraMath's shrinking window mechanic, which actively accelerates the pressure. Math Flash's timer is a fixed threshold, not a shrinking one. This distinction is worth making explicit in FAQ copy.
- **Boredom + anxiety is a toxic combination.** Multiple reviews name both boredom (repetitive, dull) and anxiety (timer, stress). These students are not engaged enough to try and not comfortable enough to relax. Math Flash's design addresses both: Practice Quest breaks the monotony at the moment of struggle, and no score pressure removes the anxiety component.
- **"The more fun the learning is, the better you will learn"** — a student who cites science to defend their position is remarkably self-aware. The homeschool parent reading reviews will notice this.
- **Institutional vs. chosen.** Multiple reviews note "my teacher makes us use it" or "my class has to do XtraMath." The resentment is partly about compulsion. A tool a parent *chooses* for their child, with the child's participation, starts from a fundamentally different emotional baseline.
